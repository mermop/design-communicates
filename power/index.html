Design communicates power.

To design is to manipulate the world around us. This is a type of power. Who gets to wield it, the degree of freedom they have, and the degree of influence the designs have, is reflective of power relationships.

Economic power determines what gets design expertise spent on it; designers work within constraints defined by clients, or designers with the economic luxury of free time define their own constraints.

Power relations are ubiquitous and complex, and their effect can be difficult to isolate. They range from overt - the relationship between a junior designer and a senior designer - to subtle - a hiring manager considering two candidates with similar qualifications and different genders or ethnicities.

To design at scale, to design as a practise, to design as a job, is to negotiate power relations on an interpersonal and societal level. To design responsibly, we need to be aware of the power structures we are part of.

  • What gets made? What gets shot down in the boardroom?
  • Who solicits work? Who says no? Who is paying you?
  • Who gets space to be creative? Who implements without getting creative input?
  • Whose opinion is asked? Whose opinion is offered?
  • Who gets designed for? Who is marginalised?
  • What is valued? What is ignored?
