Design communicates.

Design invariably serves the purpose of conveying messages, values, and expectations - whether as its primary function, as in graphic design, or as well as its function, as in industrial design. As with any communication, it always contains traces of its origins and intentions encoded within it.

To design well is to communicate effectively and authentically. Good design harmonises embedded messages with intended messages, and considers the context it will be interpreted within. While a designer can never have complete control over the interpretation of a work, especially one that will be read by many people in many contexts, it is crucial to consider the likely effects of context on its interpreted message. Dissonance between explicit and implicit messages undermines authenticity.

Design communicates culture.

Design elements are inherently culturally encoded and decoded.

Our own cultural context leads us to design with particular visual, textual and experiential shorthands and expectations. People then interpret based on their own cultural experiences, which may translate the same shorthands into something different.

  • To design for resonance in specific groups, we should leverage cultural shorthands.
  • To design for many, we need to view how our designs will be interpreted through other peoples’ cultural experiences.

Design communicates power.

To design is to manipulate the world around us. This is a type of power. Who gets to wield it, the degree of freedom they have, and the degree of influence the designs have, is reflective of power relationships.

Economic power determines what gets design expertise spent on it. Clients constrain the scope of design projects, or designers with the economic luxury of free time define their own constraints.

Power relations are ubiquitous and complex, and their effect can be difficult to isolate. They range from overt - the relationship between a junior designer and a senior designer - to subtle - the effects of personal friendships and relationships when considering a designer's opinion.

As Foucault tells us in the History of Sexuality, power relationships exist and operate within other types of relationships, and play a directly productive role in negotiation and decisionmaking.

Design involves making a series of decisions. Design within any organisation involves a series of negotiations related to these decisions. Power relationships are intimately involved here.

Conway's Law speaks of systems design reproducing communication structures:

organizations which design systems ... are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations

Similarly, design output is reflective of the power structures of organisations and societies.

To design at scale, to design as a practise, to design as a job, is to negotiate power relations on an interpersonal and societal level. To design responsibly, we need to be aware of the power structures we are part of.
